Preface

A topic reference for biomedical research and peer review.

Why this book exists

The R/biostatistics shelf is crowded — Moscarelli’s Biostatistics With ‘R’, Srinivasan’s R for Basic Biostatistics in Medical Research, Harrell’s Biostatistics for Biomedical Research, Shahbaba’s Biostatistics with R, the Cambridge text, the Springer epidemiology volume, and Lepš & Šmilauer on the ecological side. They are good books. They are also linear textbooks: open at page one, work to the end.

This book is shaped differently, around two readers it expects to serve in the same week:

  1. The author who has data in front of them and needs to pick a method, run it correctly in R, and write the Methods paragraph.
  2. The peer reviewer who has a manuscript on their desk and needs to know — for the method the authors used — what a competent application looks like, which diagnostics should have been reported, and what red flags to flag.

Every method page therefore ends with a For Reviewers section: the misapplications, the missing diagnostics, the red flags in tables and figures, and the things to verify against the authors’ references and arithmetic. The book opens at Chapter 1, a decision tree from research question to method, not at a glossy introduction. A final part, Reporting Templates, gives copy-paste Methods + Results paragraphs tagged with STROBE / CONSORT / TRIPOD-AI / PRISMA / ARRIVE.

Provenance and attribution

The substantive content is consolidated and re-organised from two of my earlier public, MIT-licensed repositories that served as inspiration:

  • CTTIR/courses — https://github.com/CTTIR/courses. A set of hands-on lab materials. The labs have been re-homed by topic in this book rather than reproduced in their original sequence.
  • CTTIR/tutorials — https://github.com/CTTIR/tutorials. A collection of method pages written to a nine-section template. The template is preserved and extended here with a For Reviewers tenth section.

Two pieces of original material are added on top:

  • The For Reviewers sections, written specifically for this volume.
  • The Reporting Templates part, which aggregates Methods + Results paragraphs by reporting guideline.

A decision-tree wizard remains live at https://cttir.github.io/tutorials/decision-tree/ — Chapter 0 mirrors the static tree and links out to the wizard for clickable navigation.

Sibling volume

This is the second volume in the …in R series. The first, on a different topic, is r-heller/strategy-in-r — Strategy in R: Game Theory, Simulation, and Machine Intelligence. The two share branding, build conventions (bookdown, renv, GitHub Pages deploy), and a preference for R-only worked examples over toy abstractions.

How to read

If you already know the method, jump to its chapter. If not, start at Chapter 1. If you are reviewing a paper, search for the method by name and read the For Reviewers section first.
